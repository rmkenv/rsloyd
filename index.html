Slow, useful,
one piece at a time.

Sloyd (from the Swedish slöjd) is a Scandinavian tradition built around making useful, beautiful objects entirely by hand. Every spoon on this bench starts life as a single length of green wood — split along the grain, never sawn — and is shaped with nothing more than an axe and a hook knife before it ever meets a piece of sandpaper.

No two pieces come out the same. That's not a flaw in the process — it's the whole point of it.

From log to table

  1. Log

    A fresh-cut branch or small log, chosen for the grain and the natural bend it wants to become.

  2. Split

    Halved and quartered along the grain with a froe or axe, so the fibers run the full length of the spoon.

  3. Rough-out

    Axe work clears the bulk of the waste and blocks in the silhouette — bowl, neck, and handle.

  4. Carve

    Hook knife and straight sloyd knife shape the bowl and handle while the wood is still green and soft to cut.

  5. Dry

    Slow, even air-drying keeps the piece from cracking as moisture leaves the wood.

  6. Finish

    A food-safe oil, usually flaxseed or walnut, seals the surface and brings the grain up.
